Common Questions

Find quick answers to your most frequent dental concerns.

What makes Sai Nivedhan Dental Care different from other clinics? ▼
Sai Nivedhan Dental Care is led by an MDS‑qualified Periodontist and Implantologist — a specialist trained extensively in gum health, implants, and complex restorative procedures. This means your treatments aren't just routine; they're planned and executed with advanced clinical expertise that general dentists are not formally trained for. Combined with modern technology, strict sterilization, and a patient‑first approach, the clinic delivers care that is both precise and comfortable. With a consistent track record of 5-star reviews, patients consistently highlight painless treatments, clear explanations, and a warm, family‑friendly environment.
Who will handle my case? ▼
Your treatment is handled personally by Dr. Akshaya Narayanan, the lead doctor of the clinic. She doesn't delegate your care — she evaluates your condition herself, explains every step clearly, and ensures that each procedure is carried out with precision and calm, steady attention. Patients consistently appreciate that she takes the time to understand their concerns, works gently, and stays fully involved from diagnosis to follow‑up. When you visit, you're not passed from one dentist to another — Dr. Akshaya is the one guiding and overseeing your entire treatment journey.
What can I expect in a single dental visit? ▼
Many procedures — such as fillings, scaling, polishing, simple extractions, and some crowns — can be completed in a single visit. Treatments like root canals, implants, periodontal therapy, and full‑mouth rehabilitation may require multiple visits depending on complexity. Digital tools like intraoral cameras and CAD/CAM workflows help speed up diagnosis and reduce the number of appointments needed.
What kinds of complex procedures do you handle? ▼
The clinic has handled several advanced cases, including: full mouth rehabilitation with implants, complex wisdom tooth surgeries, advanced periodontal treatments, multi‑tooth implant placements, and restorations for severely damaged or missing teeth. These cases are managed with careful planning, modern technology, and specialist expertise.
How do I know if I need to see a periodontist? ▼
You may need a periodontist if you're noticing signs that your gums or supporting bone need specialist attention — things like bleeding while brushing, persistent bad breath, gum recession, loose teeth, or gaps forming between teeth. If you've been advised to consider implants, or if your gum issues keep returning despite regular cleanings, a specialist evaluation can give you a clearer picture of what's happening beneath the surface. A periodontist focuses specifically on the foundation that holds your teeth in place, so seeing one early can help prevent small concerns from becoming bigger problems.
Are dental implants safe? How long do they last? ▼
Dental implants are considered one of the safest and most predictable treatments in modern dentistry. They're made from biocompatible materials that integrate naturally with your bone, and when placed with proper planning and sterilization, the procedure is very safe. As for longevity, implants are designed to last many years — often decades — as long as the surrounding gums and bone stay healthy.
Does getting a dental implant hurt? ▼
The procedure itself is done under local anaesthesia, so you won't feel pain during the placement — most patients are surprised by how comfortable it is. What some people experience afterward is mild soreness, slight swelling, or tenderness around the area for a few days, which is normal healing and manageable with standard pain relief. The idea that implant surgery is intensely painful is largely a fear built on outdated experiences. With modern techniques and proper anaesthesia, it's typically no more uncomfortable than a tooth extraction. We will also give you clear post-procedure instructions so recovery is as smooth as possible.
What are CAD/CAM Zirconia crowns, and why are they better? ▼
A crown is a cap that covers a damaged or weakened tooth to restore its shape, strength, and appearance. Traditionally, crowns were made by sending impressions to an external lab, which meant waiting days or even weeks for the final result — and sometimes dealing with poorly fitting crowns that needed adjustments. CAD/CAM (Computer-Aided Design / Computer-Aided Manufacturing) changes that entirely. A digital scan of your tooth is taken — no messy impression putty — and a computer precisely designs the crown to match the exact dimensions of your tooth. It's then milled from a solid block of Zirconia, which is a high-strength ceramic material.

Why does that matter to you? Zirconia crowns are tooth-coloured and look completely natural. They're extremely strong — resistant to chipping and cracking — and are metal-free, which means no dark lines at the gum, no allergic reactions, and no long-term discolouration. Because the fit is digitally precise, they last significantly longer than conventionally made crowns and require fewer adjustments. For front teeth, the aesthetics are excellent. For back teeth, the strength is unmatched.
I'm scared of dental procedures. Is that normal? ▼
Completely normal — dental anxiety is one of the most common reasons people delay treatment, and it's something the clinic takes seriously. Dr. Akshaya takes a calm, unhurried approach: explaining what's about to happen before doing it, checking in during the procedure, and never rushing through a step. Most patients who come in nervous leave saying the experience was far gentler than they expected. If you have a low pain threshold or a specific fear, just mention it at the start — the team will adjust the pace and technique accordingly.
My child needs dental treatment. Do you see children? ▼
Yes. The clinic sees patients across all age groups, including children. Early dental visits are actually important — they help catch issues like misaligned teeth, decay, or crowding before they become harder to correct. Children are treated gently and patiently, and the clinic's calm, friendly environment helps first-time young visitors feel at ease rather than anxious.
How often should I visit the dentist even if nothing seems wrong? ▼
Twice a year is the general recommendation — once every six months. Most dental problems, including cavities, gum disease, and early-stage bone loss, don't cause pain or visible symptoms until they've progressed significantly. A routine check-up and cleaning every six months allows the doctor to catch and address issues early, when treatment is simpler, less invasive, and less expensive. Think of it less as fixing a problem and more as preventing one from forming.
My gums bleed when I brush. Is that serious? ▼
Bleeding gums are not normal, even if it happens occasionally. It's usually the earliest sign of gingivitis — inflammation caused by plaque buildup along the gum line. At this stage, it's entirely reversible with a professional cleaning and improved brushing technique. However, if left unaddressed, gingivitis can progress into periodontitis — a deeper infection that affects the bone supporting your teeth. That's significantly harder to treat and can eventually lead to tooth loss. The reassuring part is that if you're catching it at the bleeding stage, you're catching it early. A visit now is far simpler than waiting until the symptoms get worse.
What is periodontal treatment, and will I need surgery? ▼
Periodontal treatment addresses disease affecting the gums and the bone that supports your teeth. The approach depends entirely on how far the disease has progressed. In early to moderate cases, a non-surgical procedure called scaling and root planing is used — a deep cleaning that removes bacterial deposits from below the gum line and smooths the root surfaces so the gums can reattach properly. Most patients respond well to this and don't need anything further. Surgery is considered only when pockets between the gums and teeth are too deep to clean non-surgically, or when bone loss needs to be addressed directly. Even then, periodontal surgery today is far less involved than it once was, and recovery is generally manageable. Whether or not surgery is needed, Dr. Akshaya will explain exactly what's happening in your mouth and what the least invasive path forward looks like.
Can I get all my dental work done in one place? ▼
The clinic handles a wide range of treatments under one roof — from routine cleanings and fillings to implants, periodontal surgery, root canals, cosmetic work, orthodontics, and complex restorations. The intention is to be a complete dental home so that patients aren't bounced between multiple clinics for different procedures. In the rare instance that a case requires a subspecialist outside the clinic's scope, you'll be informed clearly and referred appropriately — but for the vast majority of dental needs, everything can be managed here.
How long does a root canal take, and will it hurt? ▼
A root canal has a reputation that far exceeds its actual discomfort. The procedure is done under local anaesthesia, and most patients feel nothing beyond a mild pressure sensation. The goal of a root canal is actually to relieve pain — the tooth being treated is often already causing significant discomfort due to infection, so the procedure brings relief, not the other way around. In terms of time, a straightforward single-rooted tooth can be completed in one visit of about 45 to 60 minutes. Multi-rooted teeth or more complex cases may be split across two visits. Afterward, the tooth is usually capped with a crown to protect it long-term. Modern rotary instruments and digital imaging have made the entire process faster, more predictable, and far more comfortable than what people typically imagine.
Are braces the only option for crooked teeth? ▼
Traditional metal braces are effective, but they're not the only route. Clear aligners — like Invisalign or similar systems — are a popular alternative for adults and older teenagers who want to straighten their teeth without visible brackets and wires. They're removable, so eating and cleaning your teeth remains normal, and they're nearly invisible in day-to-day life. For very minor crowding or spacing issues, cosmetic options like veneers or bonding can sometimes achieve the appearance of straighter teeth without any orthodontic movement at all. The right choice depends on the complexity of your case, your lifestyle, and your preference. We will assess your bite and alignment and walk you through which options are genuinely suitable for your situation — not just what's trending.
I've been told I need a tooth extraction. Can it be replaced? ▼
Yes — and planning the replacement before the extraction is actually the smarter approach. When a tooth is removed, the bone beneath it begins to shrink over time because there's no longer a root stimulating it. If you wait too long before getting an implant or other replacement, that bone loss can complicate or increase the cost of future treatment. The most natural and durable replacement is a dental implant — a titanium root placed into the jaw with a crown on top. It looks, feels, and functions like a real tooth. Other options include a fixed bridge (which anchors to adjacent teeth) or a removable partial denture. Each has its trade-offs in terms of longevity, cost, and impact on surrounding teeth. At your consultation, the options will be laid out clearly so you can make an informed decision before proceeding with the extraction.
How do I know if my child needs braces? ▼
Parents often notice crowding or crooked teeth, but there are subtler signs worth watching too — difficulty chewing, mouth breathing, thumb sucking beyond age five, early or late loss of baby teeth, or jaws that shift when your child bites down. An orthodontic evaluation is typically recommended around age seven, not because treatment necessarily starts then, but because that's when a specialist can identify how the adult teeth are coming in and whether early guidance would prevent more complex treatment later. Catching issues early often means shorter, simpler treatment down the line.
What's the difference between scaling and a regular cleaning? ▼
A regular cleaning — what's done during a routine check-up — removes plaque and surface stains from above the gum line. It's preventive and should ideally be done every six months. Scaling goes deeper. It removes hardened tartar (calcified deposits that regular brushing can't touch) from both above and below the gum line. If your gums are inflamed, bleeding, or if it's been a while since your last dental visit, scaling is typically needed before the gums can return to a healthy state. It's not painful — a mild sensitivity or tickling sensation is what most people experience. For patients with early gum disease, it's often the only treatment needed to bring things back under control.
Is teeth whitening safe, and how long does it last? ▼
Professional teeth whitening done at a dental clinic is safe when performed correctly. The whitening agent used is a controlled concentration of hydrogen peroxide or carbamide peroxide, applied carefully to avoid contact with the gums. Some people experience temporary sensitivity for a day or two afterward, which fades on its own. How long it lasts depends on lifestyle — coffee, tea, red wine, and tobacco are the main culprits that cause staining to return. On average, professional whitening lasts anywhere from one to three years with normal habits. Touch-ups can be done periodically to maintain the result. It's worth noting that whitening works on natural teeth only — it won't change the colour of crowns, veneers, or fillings, so those would need to be factored in if you're planning both whitening and restorative work.
